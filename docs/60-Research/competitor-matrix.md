# Competitor Matrix

Comparative analysis of the football management products most likely to anchor
user expectations for soccer-manager. Focus is on actionable product scope and
differentiation, not implementation detail. Club Boss is referenced here at a
profile level only and treated in depth in [[club-boss-analysis]].

> Wave 2 (2026-05-16) added rows for ManagerZone, SOKKA, Soccer Manager 2026
> and EA Fußball Manager. The deeper competitor inventory now lives in
> [[feature-library-synthesis]]. This matrix keeps the original side-by-side
> product view.
> Retrieval date for all sources: **2026-05-15** (Wave 1) and
> **2026-05-16** (Wave 2). See [Sources](#sources).

## Method

For each competitor we capture how a manager-genre player would perceive it:
platforms, business model, whether they can keep playing without a network,
how deep the simulation feels, whether the mobile experience is first-class,
plus a one-line strength and weakness. The second table maps coverage of the
classic manager pillars (league, transfers, training, youth, stadium, finance,
match simulation, save/cloud). Both tables use the same product order so they
can be read side by side.

Legend for coverage: **Deep** = signature feature, **Solid** = present and
playable, **Light** = present but shallow/abstracted, **Out** = not in product,
**N/A** = not applicable to that business model.

## Differentiation opportunities for soccer-manager

The matrix surfaces a clear gap between *deep desktop simulations that are
hostile to mobile and offline play* (Football Manager, Anstoss, We Are
Football, FIFA Manager) and *mobile-first products that are shallow or
online-only* (Top Eleven, OSM, Soccer Manager, partly Club Boss). soccer-manager
can target the empty quadrant: **deep, offline-first, mobile-friendly,
non-predatory**.

- **Offline-first is genuinely rare.** Only Anstoss, FM (post-activation), We
  Are Football, EA FuMa, and Club Boss let you keep playing without a network,
  and only Club Boss is mobile-first. With Workbox + Dexie + a local match
  engine we own this dimension by default. See [[../60-Research/pwa-offline-patterns]]
  and ADR-0002 Offline-first.
- **Mobile depth.** Mobile competitors lean on F2P loops and currencies; nobody
  ships Football-Manager-grade depth on a phone. A PWA that compresses an
  Anstoss-style sim into a touch UI is unclaimed territory.
- **No predatory monetisation.** Top Eleven, OSM, Soccer Manager, and Club Boss
  all rely on premium currency and accelerators. A one-time-paid or
  cosmetic-only PWA differentiates strongly on review/word-of-mouth.
- **No real-IP dependence.** FM26, SM24, OSM, and Top Eleven all advertise real
  player and club licences; that arms race is unwinnable for an indie. The
  fictional naming schema (ADR-0007) is therefore aligned with our IP risk
  profile in [[ip-and-licensing]] and can be reframed as a feature ("your own
  football universe") instead of a compromise.
- **German-language management humor.** Anstoss owns this niche in spirit but
  ships an unstable product; We Are Football inherits the lineage but is
  desktop-only. A PWA with a strong de-DE voice fills a recognisable gap.
- **Anstoss-style "Bundestrainer" career arc.** FM has national teams but they
  are a side activity; Anstoss made the journey from club coach to national
  coach the spine of the game. Re-using that arc in a PWA gives the game a
  narrative anchor competitors lack at this granularity.
- **Async hotseat / pass-and-play.** We Are Football is the only modern
  competitor with hotseat multiplayer; on a phone, pass-and-play is trivial to
  implement with Dexie-backed saves and is a fresh angle in the genre.
- **Save portability via export.** No competitor offers user-owned saves you
  can export, version, or share. A "download your career" feature is cheap to
  build on Dexie + JSON and signals trust.

## MVP-expectation risks

Where competitors have anchored expectations the MVP can credibly miss only if
we frame the scope explicitly. The risks below should feed
[[../95-Archive/gap-reports/feature-gap-analysis]] when we pick the MoSCoW list.

- **Match visualisation.** FM26, Top Eleven, Soccer Manager, EA FuMa, and We
  Are Football all advertise 3D match views. A text/2D MVP must signal this is
  a stylistic, Anstoss-style choice, not a missing feature.
- **Real player and club licences.** FM26, SM24, and OSM lean on real names.
  ADR-0007 keeps us fictional, which we must merchandise as a feature (custom
  universe, no licensing churn) rather than apologise for.
- **Live online leagues / async PvP.** Top Eleven and OSM trained an audience
  to expect competing against real managers. MVP single-player play needs to
  ship with hotseat or async friend leagues on the roadmap, or churn risk
  rises.
- **Cloud save / multi-device.** FM, Top Eleven, OSM, and Soccer Manager all
  offer "log in elsewhere and continue." Pure offline Dexie storage looks
  regressive without an explicit export/import (and later opt-in cloud sync)
  story.
- **Women's football.** FM26 and We Are Football 2024 ship women's competitions
  as a headline. Skipping it at MVP is acceptable, but the data model should
  not preclude it (see ADR-0004 Data Model).
- **Stadium and finance depth.** Anstoss, EA FuMa, and We Are Football set a
  high bar on stadium/finance gameplay; mobile audiences from Top Eleven and
  Club Boss expect at least an upgrade tree. MVP needs *some* stadium economy,
  not none.
- **Annual update cadence.** SM24/25 and the FM line condition players to
  expect yearly data refreshes. Living off a fictional universe lets us ship
  on a slower content cadence, but we should communicate that explicitly.
- **Tutorialisation.** Top Eleven, OSM, and SM24 all front-load onboarding;
  Anstoss is widely criticised for confusing menus. MVP UX must beat Anstoss
  on first-session clarity to avoid sharing its reputation.
