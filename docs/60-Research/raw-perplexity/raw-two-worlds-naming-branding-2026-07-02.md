# Raw capture — Two-worlds naming & mode branding (FMX-213)

Raw external-research capture for the FMX-213 fork: what to name the two
player-facing "worlds" (DE-first, IP-clean, co-equal, no easy/hard collision),
how to present the choice at onboarding, and how to frame both so neither reads
as the lesser game. Verbatim-substance answers + source URLs below; graded for
confidence. This note is a capture, not a decision.

---

## Q1 — Purpose/identity-framed difficulty & mode names: what reduces stigma vs reads as "the lesser mode"? (Perplexity Sonar)

**Substance of answer.** Identity- or purpose-framed names — labels that
describe the player's *goal* rather than their *skill* — are the pattern
coverage treats as reducing stigma and improving self-selection. Labels that
sound like "the real game" or "the lesser mode" carry prestige/shame cues,
especially when one option is framed as the default/true experience.

- **God of War (2018)** — exact labels: **"Give Me a Story" / "Give Me a
  Balanced Experience" / "Give Me a Challenge" / "Give Me God of War."**
  Game Informer describes "Give Me a Story" as for players who want the
  narrative with "a lot of leeway," and "Give Me a Balanced Experience" as a
  middle option "not too hard or easy." Den of Geek reads the four as an
  explicit ladder (story-breeze → intended → veteran → punishing). Caveat the
  model raised: the "Give Me ___" verb-framing is more expressive and less
  morally loaded than easy/normal/hard, but the four still form a *ladder*.
- **Marvel's Spider-Man** — label **"Friendly Neighborhood"** (lowest of
  Friendly Neighborhood / Friendly / Amazing / Spectacular / Ultimate). Reads
  as inviting/personality-forward rather than "Easy," which softens stigma —
  but coverage says players still parse it as the easy tier because the labels
  are clearly *ordered*; the higher labels sound "more real/heroic." Lesson:
  flavour reduces stigma but does not create co-equality when labels obviously
  scale by skill. (Model flagged this as general-coverage synthesis, not a
  single formal UX source → medium confidence.)
- **Fire Emblem — "Classic" vs "Casual"** (Classic = permadeath; Casual =
  fallen units return). Coverage/community consistently read **"Classic" as
  "the real / traditional / intended" experience** and "Casual" as an
  accessibility/newcomer concession — even when devs insist both are valid, the
  *name* "Classic" implies authenticity. This is the canonical
  co-equality-undermined-by-naming example. (medium — community/coverage
  synthesis.)
- **Celeste — "Assist Mode"** — framed as an accessibility tool, not a lesser
  difficulty; avoids implying the "real" game is elsewhere. (Sonar could not
  cite reception from its result set → treat reception as low-confidence here;
  the *copy-patch* fact is documented in the sibling mode-choice note.)
- **Hades — "God Mode"** — purpose/empowerment-framed help feature (accrues
  damage resistance over deaths); the external, joke-power framing lands as
  empowering rather than "you are weak." (reception low-confidence in this set.)
- **Wolfenstein II — "Can I play, Daddy?"** (lowest tier, baby-bonnet art) —
  deliberately self-deprecating; the clearest example of a label that does NOT
  read as a neutral co-equal world; comically-easy, identity-signalling.

**Design takeaway captured:** for *two co-equal worlds*, avoid any label that
encodes skill, deficiency or completeness in the wording; prefer labels that are
*orthogonal* (describe a different session/experience) rather than scaled.

**Sources:**
- https://gameinformer.com/b/news/archive/2018/04/10/god-of-war-difficulty-modes-and-customization-outlined.aspx
- https://www.denofgeek.com/games/god-of-war-best-difficulty-setting/
- https://godofwar.fandom.com/wiki/God_Mode
**Confidence:** high for GoW exact labels + reading (primary/secondary coverage);
medium for Spider-Man / Fire Emblem readings (coverage synthesis); low for
Celeste/Hades/Wolfenstein reception in *this* result set (labels themselves are
high-confidence, widely documented).

## Q2 — German (DE) mode/difficulty naming: which words carry casual stigma vs neutral/prestige feel; evocative football-manager terms (Perplexity Sonar)

**Substance of answer.** German localizations lean on three neutral/prestigious
naming families — **role/identity words** (Trainer, Manager, Spielmacher,
Stratege), **context/place words** (Spieltag, Trainerbank, Kabine, Taktiktafel),
and **metaphor words** (Masterplan, Kommandozentrale, Feldherr). Explicitly
evaluative labels (Einsteiger, Profi, Experte, Anfänger, Casual, Arcade) imply
hierarchy/stigma. FM's German site itself avoids beginner/profi framing —
markets "eine frische Manager-Erfahrung" and "die Bühne, auf der du deine
Fußball-Geschichte inszenierst" (story/role over difficulty).

Term-by-term connotation grading captured:

- **Strongly co-equal / neutral-prestige:** **Spieltag** (matchday atmosphere,
  live emotion, tempo — "am Puls des Spiels", *not* casual); **Trainerbank**
  (touchline, in-the-moment reactive decisions; very football-native);
  **Taktiktafel** (whiteboard, concrete planning image); **Taktikzentrale /
  Kommandozentrale** ("HQ"/overview/systems, high-level mastermind vibe);
  **Kabine** (man-management, dressing-room, emotional-but-serious);
  **Masterplan** (planning/control, mildly heroic, not technically elitist);
  **Spielmacher** (playmaker; shapes the game; prestigious but not
  expert-gated); **Chefsache** ("the boss's own matter" — importance/personal
  involvement, not difficulty); **Bauchgefühl / Instinkt** (intuitive decisions;
  co-equal *if* contrasted with Analyse/Masterplan — does imply less analysis).
- **Risk of hierarchy / "lesser" or "more serious" skew:** **Schnellzugriff**
  (UX/menu word → reads "shortcut/abridged", not a world); **Schnellspiel**
  (widely used for quick-match modes → inherently "less deep" than a full
  career mode); **Feldherr / Kommandostand** (militaristic, "genius general" →
  reads as the *more prestigious/pro* identity, breaks co-equality); **Profi /
  Experte / Einsteiger / Arcade / Casual** (direct difficulty ladder / stigma).

**Concrete co-equal DE pairs the model surfaced:**
1. **„Spieltag"** (Matchday-Erlebniswelt) / **„Taktiktafel"** (Planungs- &
   Kontrollwelt) — perspective-based, football-native, neither implies
   easier/harder.
2. **„Bauchgefühl"** (Instinkt-Coach) / **„Masterplan"** (Konzept-Coach) —
   identity/philosophy contrast (Intuition vs Konzeption), both prestigious in
   football talk ("Erfolgstrainer vertraut seinem Bauchgefühl").
3. **„Trainerbank"** / **„Taktikzentrale"** — in-the-moment vs holistic control;
   both sound managerial and serious.

Recommendation captured (model's, not a decision): frame as **„Trainerwelten"**
reflecting coaching *style*, put any "short vs long session" hint in the
description, never in the world name.

**Sources:**
- https://www.footballmanager.com/de
- https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fu%C3%9Fball_Manager
- https://fm.zweierkette.de/kompendium/fm-spielerrollen-liste/
- https://www.meistertrainerforum.de/index.php?topic=1408.0
**Confidence:** medium — connotation grading is native-speaker/coverage
synthesis (no controlled study); the FM.de marketing-copy framing is
high-confidence (primary page). Every candidate still needs the GD-0015 /
ADR-0007 IP-clean denylist + confusable check before use.

## Q3 — Game-design writing on naming difficulty modes to avoid stigma / "normal-implies-default" bias (Exa web_search)

**Substance / key sources found:**

- **Cogmind / Grid Sage Games, "Rebranding Difficulty Modes" (Kyzrati,
  2019-09-17; mirrored on Game Developer 2019-09-27).** First-hand designer
  account: the original scheme "emphasiz[ed] the 'proper' way to play, and
  everything else is below that … people didn't really want to proactively
  select lower modes due to how they're named." Rebranded to evocative,
  non-loaded names **Explorer / Adventurer / Rogue**. Core mechanism quote:
  *"Would you rather lower the difficulty setting from the default to 'easier,'
  or switch from Rogue to Adventurer? Even if the end result is the same, for
  some people the answer is different for each question, regardless of ability
  or need!"* Also: naming one option **"normal" in the middle "would imply it's
  the standard/intended way to play, when it really isn't"** — and letting
  players make an *informed choice up front* beats defaulting high and asking
  them to lower it later ("harder for them to stomach"). Direct support for
  purpose-framed, no-"Normal", choose-up-front onboarding.
  URL: https://www.gridsagegames.com/blog/2019/09/rebranding-difficulty-modes/
  (mirror: https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/rebranding-difficulty-modes)
- **AUTOMATON WEST, "Game developers wonder if it's time to move away from the
  term 'Easy Mode'" (2022-06-01).** Documents the industry trend away from
  easy/normal/hard; "the term 'easy' seems to leave a bad taste"; stigma comes
  from "glorification of beating difficult games … a hierarchy of gamers." Notes
  a Twitter poll where 90% said "Easy Mode" doesn't bother them — but flags
  selection bias (poll skews hardcore, likely misses the players who'd pick
  Easy). Useful nuance: the stigma is real but unevenly felt.
  URL: https://automaton-media.com/en/news/20220601-13021/
- **Game Developer, "The Difficulty Dilemma Part 1" & "Difficulty Levels And
  Why You Should Never Use Them" (2016).** "DON'T SHAME YOUR PLAYERS FOR
  CHOOSING A LOWER DIFFICULTY"; difficulty labels "can make players feel
  inadequate … challenge their preconceived identity as video game players";
  players "often just quit rather than mess with difficulty settings." Argues
  descriptive-beyond-label copy (what actually changes) and never gating
  content/true-ending behind the top tier.
  URLs: https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/the-difficulty-dilemma-part-1 ,
  https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/difficulty-levels-and-why-you-should-never-use-them
- **Hopeful Homies, "Difficulty Modes and Framing Language in Games" (2016).**
  Mass Effect 2's story/combat/mix split cited as the strong positive pattern:
  it "genuinely identifies that gamers have different reasons for playing" and
  maps the *experience sought*, not skill. Guidance: modes should be
  "descriptive beyond their labels," validate the player's choice, never
  judgmental.
  URL: https://hopefulhomies.com/2016/11/03/homie-bites-difficulty-modes-and-framing-language-in-games/

**Confidence:** high — Cogmind is a primary designer post; the Game Developer /
AUTOMATON pieces are reputable industry coverage. This is the strongest external
grounding for "choose-up-front, purpose-framed, no-'Normal', don't shame."

## Q4 — Onboarding UX: presenting two CO-EQUAL choice cards (equal weight, no "recommended", middle-option risk) + Spider-Man / Fire Emblem readings (Perplexity Sonar)

**Substance of answer.** Two co-equal experience options work best framed as
*different ways to enjoy the same game*, with genuinely equal visual weight,
purpose-based labels, and copy that avoids implying one is default/"normal"/more
"real." As soon as "Normal," "Recommended," or "Classic/real way" framing
appears, players infer hierarchy and self-sort by perceived skill/validity
rather than desired experience.

Concrete UX rules captured:
- **Equal visual weight:** same size/shape/container/padding/icon-presence/
  elevation for both cards (segmented-control guidance: equal-importance options
  share dimensions). Order for clarity is OK; styling must stay neutral — no
  accent color, bigger label, or "default" badge on one.
- **No pre-selection / no "Recommended" badge** when the goal is unbiased
  self-selection ("harness the power of suggestion" works against you here); if
  guidance is needed, put it in small neutral *copy* ("Not sure? Many players
  start with …"), never as visual weight on one card.
- **Never name one option "Normal"/"Standard" in a two-option layout** — it
  brands the other as deviant. Avoid "Easy/Hard," "Beginner/Expert," "Lite,"
  "Classic/Casual," "Recommended."
- **Labels ≤2–3 words describing experience** + a one-line "who it's for / what
  changes" subtitle framed on *preference* ("enjoy the narrative" / "enjoy
  challenge"), not *ability*.
- **Reassure reversibility:** a neutral helper line — "Choose how you'd like to
  experience this game. You can change this anytime in Settings." — reduces
  lock-in anxiety.
- **Spider-Man "Friendly Neighborhood":** flavour reduces stigma but the label
  still clearly maps to "easy" because the tiers are ordered → not co-equal.
- **Fire Emblem Classic/Casual:** even with equal button size/placement, "Classic"
  linguistically claims tradition/canon and "Casual" reads as a concession →
  naming alone undermines co-equality. Avoid this shape unless you *intend* a
  canonical-vs-accessible split.

**Sources (UX-pattern guidance):**
- https://www.eleken.co/blog-posts/segmented-control-ui
- https://www.uxtweak.com/research/toggle-button-design/
- https://uxdesign.cc/selection-controls-ui-component-series-3badc0bdb546
- https://blog.prototypr.io/how-to-choose-and-design-selection-controls-in-ux-ba4a68344118
**Confidence:** high for the UX-component principles (established pattern
guidance); medium for the Spider-Man/Fire Emblem readings (coverage synthesis,
not a formal study).

## Cross-cutting notes for the synthesis

- Convergent, well-sourced principle across Q1/Q3/Q4: **purpose/identity framing
  + choose-up-front + no "Normal"/"Recommended"/"lesser-edition" language** is
  the documented anti-stigma pattern. Cogmind (Rogue/Adventurer/Explorer) and
  God of War ("Give Me ___") are the cleanest positive precedents; Fire Emblem
  Classic/Casual and Spider-Man's ordered flavour tiers are the cautions that
  even nice names fail if the pair still *reads* as a skill ladder.
- The FMX advantage the evidence points at: FMX's two worlds are **orthogonal
  session-experiences** (matchday-feel vs deep-control), *not* a skill ladder —
  which is exactly the shape (Mass Effect story/combat/mix; GoW purpose verbs)
  that survives the co-equality test. The DE pair should encode *session
  identity*, never depth/skill.
- IP-clean posture: candidate words (Spieltag, Trainerbank, Taktiktafel,
  Taktikzentrale, Bauchgefühl, Masterplan, Instinkt) are generic German
  football/coaching vocabulary, not trademarks — but "Anstoss", "Fußball
  Manager", "Football Manager" etc. are real product marks and must be avoided
  as world names; every finalist still runs the GD-0015 / ADR-0007 denylist +
  confusable/edit-distance gate and a DE-first copy test (the German pair is the
  primary surface). This capture recommends candidates; it does not clear them.
- Unverified / low-confidence to flag downstream: Celeste/Hades/Wolfenstein
  *reception* was not citable from these result sets (labels themselves are
  well-documented); DE connotation grading is native-speaker synthesis, not a
  study; Spider-Man/Fire Emblem readings are coverage synthesis.
